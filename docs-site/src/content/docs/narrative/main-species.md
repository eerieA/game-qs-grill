## Human

Was direct descendant of old earth human. Was determined to stay near the Solar system after The Collapse.

They are very good at engineering like genetic engineering, so they can make themselve look like sibling species such as Hydrune, but that does not last long since evolved environment adaptation is a complex, multi-system change costing a lot.

## Hydrune

Was also direct descendant of old earth human. But this aquatic human variant diverged enough from neo humans to become a separate species, since they chose to settle on sea-rich planets after The Collapse, and it has been a very very long time.

### Their voices

They don't use sounds in human hearing range to communicate. Centuries of adaptation in sea environments made them evolve an ultrasound system, a bit like dolphins on old earth.

## Veydrin

### Evolution environment

The Veydrin evolved within the cloud/fluid layers of a low-gravity, gas-rich planetary atmosphere—most plausibly a gas giant or sub-Neptune. In this environment, there is no solid surface; instead, the environment consists of stratified layers containing hydrogen, helium, methane, ammonia, and suspended droplets of water or hydrocarbons.

This combination of:
- buoyant support  
- chemical richness  
- and continuous fluid motion  

selects for organisms that are **soft, distributed, and dynamically stable rather than rigid and anatomically fixed**. The Veydrin are thus best understood as coherent fluid systems adapted to persist within a constantly shifting medium.

### Metabolism

The Veydrin body plan is composed of three components: "skin", internal flow and distributed metabolic regions.

#### 1. Reactive boundary layer (“skin”)
Rather than a tangible membrane, the Veydrin possess a **diffuse, chemically active boundary layer**. This layer is enriched with specialized molecules that:
- regulate diffusion  
- maintain internal chemical gradients  
- respond dynamically to external pressure changes  

It is continuously regenerated through metabolic activity and behaves more like a **self-maintaining interface between fluids** than a biological skin.

#### 2. Internal flow structure
Internally, the Veydrin are organized around **circulatory flow patterns** rather than fixed organs. Matter and energy are transported through:
- convection-like flows  
- localized vortices  
- pressure-driven circulation  

#### 3. Distributed metabolic regions
There is no centralized "heart". Instead, the Veydrin contain **high-activity metabolic regions** (HAMR if you prefer) distributed throughout their volume. These regions:
- drive chemical reactions (e.g., redox processes involving hydrogen or methane)  
- sustain the boundary layer  
- actively maintain internal organization  

These active regions can migrate, expand, or contract depending on environmental conditions, effectively acting as a distributed, dynamically located “energy core”.

### Shape shifting

Is a natural consequence of their metabolism architecture. The Veydrin continuously adjust their morphology through:

- modulation of internal flow patterns  
- redistribution of metabolic activity  
- local variations in boundary layer chemistry  

Because their structure is dynamically maintained, their form is inherently fluid. What appears as deliberate shape shifting is often a combination of **passive response to environmental forces** and **active stabilization**.

### Mimetic phase

The Veydrin can shape shift very easily, so they utilize this feature of their bodies. They exhibit two primary morphological states:

#### Basal Morphology
A.k.a. **Basal Phase**. This is their default form: a diffuse, cloud-like structure with fluid, jellyfish-like shape. In this state, energy expenditure is optimized for stability, transport, and baseline metabolic function.

#### Adaptive Mimetic Phase (AMPh)

Under specific stimuli—such as the presence of other organisms—the Veydrin can enter an **Adaptive Mimetic Phase**. In this state, they actively reorganize their internal flows and boundary chemistry to approximate the structure of another organism.

This process is:
- energetically expensive  
- dynamically maintained  
- structurally approximate rather than exact  

Mimicry serve several purposes:
- predation or avoidance (historical)
- environmental integration  (historical)
- communication or signaling with other species (modern)

#### Limitation

The Adaptive Mimetic Phase cannot be sustained for extended periods (typically no more than 1–2 days). This limitation arises from:

- continuous diffusion eroding imposed structure  
- environmental turbulence disrupting stability  
- high metabolic cost of maintaining non-native configurations  

Without constant energy input, the imposed morphology degrades, and the organism naturally reverts to its Basal Morphology.
